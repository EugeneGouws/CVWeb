Eugene Gouws

Maths, music, and the software in between

Four careers that don’t usually meet — and the one way of working that runs through all of them.

I didn’t set out to be a problem-solver.

I set out to be a performer — singing with an a cappella group called D7, chasing gigs anywhere from Rocking the Daisies to weddings. Somewhere along the way real life had other plans, and I found myself crisscrossing the country instead, repping dog food for a living.

When my daughter was born, the road life gave way to something steadier. I relocated to East London, where — almost by accident — a teaching job found me before I’d thought to look for one, drawing on a music background I didn’t expect to use professionally again. What followed was a slow drift into mathematics and computer science, and the slow realisation that I’d been solving the same kind of problem all along, just never twice under the same name.

The Dog Food Years · Data driven

A branding detour that turned into a numbers problem.

SuperVet was my dad’s venture, started with a friend of his. When they needed a rep, I took it on — the next stretch on the road, visiting hundreds of vets across the country, and getting good enough at it that sales grew significantly. Along the way I redesigned the packaging myself, a small detour into branding that helped drive the food’s growing popularity. The product was something to be proud of selling: its regulatory standards were stricter than those applied to certain baby foods — which sounds like a joke until you check it and realise it isn’t.

Growing sales hid a quieter problem. The bag printer’s minimum order quantities didn’t match how fast each variant actually sold — so a single print run could lock up years of stock in the slow movers. The chart traces sell-through against those minimums.

Music · Outcome-based project management

From critical listening to a room built for it.

Long before any of that, my music career started at twelve, at the Drakensberg Boys’ Choir, where I learned discipline less as a rule and more as a kind of group physics: everyone solving the same problem at the same time. That sense of synergy took me around the world, from Spain to Japan to Norway.

My Master’s pulled the focus in tighter, into the technical side of music — critical listening, recording, mixing, and the unglamorous discipline of signal flow, which I’d later discover has more in common with programming logic than I expected. That grounding sparked an interest in acoustics: recording rock bands in garages, learning that the practical world rarely matches the textbook ideal. That gap eventually pushed me to design a proper studio at my current school — including the bass traps below, where a low frequency is caught, reflected, and absorbed before it can stand as a room mode.

Teaching and Coding · User convenience

A gap that looked administrative and felt structural.

Music wasn’t the only thing that followed me into the classroom. I’ve taught at two schools now, both for long stretches, covering mathematics and music in equal measure — but it was always mathematics where my heart sat, for the simple reason that it never stops asking you to solve something.

Years inside the school system showed me a gap nobody seemed to be fixing — the kind of problem that looks administrative from the outside and feels structural from the inside, the same kind I’d been solving in sales figures and choir formations without ever calling it that. The information needed to solve it lives scattered across different places, hard to make sense of and harder to see in context. So once I’d finished my degree in mathematics and computer science, I built for convenience — two tools below.

TimeView

A school’s whole timetable from one JSON file — browse by teacher, student or subject, no server, nothing leaving your machine.

  • Search a teacher or student to trace their week
  • Cascade menus drill from a class into its roster
  • Switch views: any teacher’s, student’s or subject’s full week

Kitchen Costings

Recipe costing for home bakers — paste a recipe, it parses the ingredients, prices them against a pantry, and works out cost per serving. Local-only, no accounts.

  • Paste or drop a recipe — auto-parsed into ingredients

And everything else

Growth as another problem worth solving.

Outside all that, I’m a parent of three — its own full-time job before the actual full-time job even starts. Between teaching during the day, extra maths and music lessons after school, and singing in a choir, the days run long and the late nights run later: mostly planning, problem-solving, and somehow keeping everyone mostly happy at the same time.

None of it has made me wary of work or commitment — if anything it’s done the opposite. I keep taking on new things to learn, not because I have spare time for them, but because growing in new directions is, in its own way, just another problem worth solving.

Digital & AI fluency

A tool to think with — not a substitute for thinking.

AI is at its worst when it’s used to outsource genuine intelligence — when it replaces the creativity and understanding that the work is actually for. It’s at its best as a collaborator: something to argue with, accelerate against, and hold to your own judgment. The line between the two is the whole point.
